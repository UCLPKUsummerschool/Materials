Abstracts

Testing The Class of Signal-Detection Models in Recognition Memory and Visual Working Memory

Signal-detection theory (SDT) is one of the most prominent formal accounts for describing judgments across a wide range of domains. Its core assumption is that judgements arise from an evaluation of latent-strength values sampled from continuous distributions; these latent strength values are either, in single-item judgments, compared against a prespecified response criterion or, in multi-alternative forced-choice (m-AFC) tasks, compared against each other. Typically SDT assumes that the latent distributions follows a pre-specified parametric form, typically the Gaussian (i.e. normal) distribution. Recently we (Kellen, Winiger, Dunn & Singmann, 2021) showed that SDT can be tested in its most general form without assuming a specific parametric form of the latent distributions. Furthermore, judgements made in long-term recognition memory tasks obey the constraints imposed by the class of SDT models. I will also present results from ongoing work showing that judgements made in a task popular in the domain of visual working memory, change detection, do not follow the constraints imposed by SDT. These latter results are in line with the assumption that visual working memory is capacity limited, a feature that is fundamentally at odds with the basic tenets of SDT.

Distractor suppression in attentional selection

Performing visual search tasks requires optimal attention deployment to promote targets and suppress distractors. In a series of studies, we adopted the different types of stimuli and measurements (behavior, eye tracking, EEG, and tACS), aiming to identify the critical factors and underlying mechanisms that facilitate distractor suppression in visual search tasks. The results demonstrated that the consistency of distractors and the availability of cognitive control played the key roles in successful distractor suppression.
