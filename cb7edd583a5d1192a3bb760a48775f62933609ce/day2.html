Day 2: July 9th 2024

Jiayu Zhan

PKU

Brad Love

Abstracts

Using psychophysics to understand the information processing mechanism of high-level visual cognition

Understanding how the brain processes information to make sense of the environment is a fundamental challenge in cognitive psychology and neuroscience. For instance, how do we recognise that an image is of Beijing instead of of London? Typically, we investigate this question by measuring the binary relationship between full images and behavioural response (or brain activity). In this lecture, I will introduce a psychophysical approach to reverse engineer the specific stimulus information processed in brain dynamics for given tasks — i.e, deciphering the triple relationship between stimulus information, brains and behaviours. I will start with behavioural paradigms for the modelling of task-relevant information that supports various visual categorizations of natural stimuli (e.g., face, natural scene). Then I will discuss MEG studies that track the processing of both task-relevant and -irrelevant information in dynamic brain activities to infer brain functions such as information Transfer, Reduction, Prediction, and Integration.

Embeddings of and for the mind

For decades, psychologists have used embeddings to characterise human knowledge. For example, Nancy Henley in the 1960s used multidimensional dimensional scaling (MDS) to create a semantic space for animal terms based on participants' similarity ratings. A variety of domains, including images, text, and brain measures, can be captured in embedding spaces. In this talk, I will consider how to collect very large semantic embeddings from human judgments. We can use these embeddings to evaluate how consistent models are with human judgment, including modern deep learning models. One conclusion is that models that tend to be better from an engineering standpoint are worse as models of humans. Finally, I will consider whether people, and in particular children, learn by aligning embeddings spaces across modalities in an unsupervised fashion.
